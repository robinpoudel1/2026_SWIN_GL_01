My first cyber role: SOC triager

You've got the certs and theory. Then Day 1 hits: a wall of alerts, new tools and decisions with real impact.

  • Drowning in alerts from SIEM and XDR platforms.
  • Googling error codes and Windows events you've never seen.
  • Writing tickets, chasing logs and asking “Is this normal or suspicious?”

Reality check

The gap between university labs and a production SOC is culture shock. That's normal. The goal is not to avoid it — it's to shorten it.

Think about: if your first screen tomorrow was 200 new alerts, what would you look at first?

Culture shock: movies vs SOC

What you expect

  • Terminal full of zero-days and instant shells.
  • Chasing APTs across the globe on day one.
  • Every day is a “we're under attack” war room.

What you actually see

  • Repeated failed logons, noisy scanners, weird scripts.
  • Log gaps, misconfigured agents, false positives.
  • Slow-burning incidents you piece together over days.

Good news: if you can handle this “boring” work with discipline, you're on track for the fun stuff.

Understanding the ranks

Red team: offence in the real world

Typical engagement flow:

  1. Recon: map exposed assets and tech stack.
  2. Initial access: phishing, web vulns, credential abuse.
  3. Privilege & movement: AD abuse, misconfigurations.
  4. Impact & reporting: show business risk, not just “got root”.

Tools mapped to phases:

  • Recon: nmap, amass, subfinder.
  • Web: Burp Suite, ffuf/gobuster, sqlmap.
  • AD: BloodHound, SharpHound, password spraying tools.
  • Reporting: structured findings with clear business impact and fixes.

Red team commands in the wild

A few commands you'll see in labs and entry-level pentest work (use only in legal environments).

Host & service discovery

nmap -sC -sV -oA corp-scan 10.0.5.0/24

Runs default scripts, version detection and saves results. First step in many internal tests.

Web content discovery

ffuf -u https://target/FUZZ -w wordlist.txt -mc 200,403

Finds hidden paths like admin panels or APIs behind a single hostname.

Blue team: defence in the real world

Typical day for a SOC / blue team analyst:

  • Monitor SIEM / XDR alerts and triage what matters.
  • Investigate suspicious logons, processes, network activity.
  • Contain hosts, reset accounts, block indicators.
  • Tune rules, close gaps and improve playbooks.

Core tools you'll touch:

  • SIEM: Sentinel, Splunk, QRadar — your log search brain.
  • EDR/XDR: Defender, CrowdStrike, etc. — deep endpoint visibility and response.
  • Cloud & email: CSPM tools, secure email gateways, identity protection.

Blue team cheat sheet: host triage

A few commands that show up again and again when investigating endpoints.

Linux

journalctl -u ssh.service --since "-1h"
ss -tulpn | grep LISTEN
tail -n 50 /var/log/auth.log

Recently seen SSH logins, listening services and auth failures.

Windows (PowerShell)

Get-WinEvent -LogName Security -MaxEvents 50
Get-Process | Sort-Object CPU -Descending | Select -First 5
netstat -ano | findstr ESTABLISHED

Recent security events, heavy processes and active connections.

SIEM in action: hunting noisy logons

Example of a KQL-style query to find accounts with many failed logons followed by a success. 

SecurityEvent
| where EventID in (4625, 4624)
| summarize Fails = countif(EventID == 4625),
          Successes = countif(EventID == 4624),
          LastSuccess = max(TimeGenerated)
  by Account, IPAddress
| where Fails >= 10 and Successes >= 1
| sort by Fails desc

This is the kind of thinking interviewers want: clear filters, aggregation, and a crisp hypothesis (“brute force from a single IP”), not just “I know tool X.”

Purple team & detection engineering

Purple is not a separate colour of hacker cloak. It's a feedback loop where Red and Blue teams level each other up.

  1. Pick a MITRE ATT&CK technique (e.g. malicious PowerShell).
  2. Red simulates it safely in a lab or production window.
  3. Blue checks: did SIEM/XDR see it? Did an alert fire?
  4. If not, write or tune a detection; then re-test.

Common purple tooling:

  • MITRE ATT&CK navigator for planning coverage.
  • Atomic Red Team, Invoke-Atomic, Caldera for repeatable tests.
  • Detection-as-code in SIEM / XDR for versioned rules.

Pentest lifecycle & interview mapping

Structured methodology employers listen for:

  1. Recon & threat modelling.
  2. Scanning & enumeration.
  3. Exploitation & post-exploitation.
  4. Privilege escalation & lateral movement.
  5. Reporting & remediation guidance.

How common questions map to that flow:

  • “Explain the pen testing methodology” → can you walk this lifecycle clearly?
  • “Vuln scan vs pen test?” → do you understand depth, validation and impact?
  • “Describe a time you breached a system” → do you handle ethics and lessons learned?

Essential skills & labs for 2026

Technical foundations:

  • Networking fundamentals: TCP/IP, DNS, HTTP, VPNs.
  • Linux & Windows administration and scripting.
  • Cloud basics: IAM, network security groups, logging.

Hands-on labs:

  • Hack The Box, TryHackMe, CyberDefenders, home lab.

Certifications (pick based on path):

  • Entry: CompTIA Security+.
  • Offence: eJPT, OSCP, GPEN.
  • Defence: CySA+, SC-200, cloud security certs.
  • Advanced: CISSP, OSEP, specialist GIAC tracks.

Choose your path & next steps

Most careers start in foundational roles (SOC, junior analyst, helpdesk) and then branch into Red, Blue, Purple, Cloud, DFIR and more.

Red‑curious

  • Finish HTB/THM beginner offensive paths.
  • Document 3 labs like real pentest reports.
  • Prepare to explain methodology in interviews.

Blue‑curious

  • Build a mini SIEM/XDR lab (Sysmon + free SIEM).
  • Write 2–3 detections for common attacks.
  • Practise explaining one investigation end‑to‑end.

Still exploring

  • Strengthen networking, OS and scripting basics.
  • Try both attack and defence labs to see what fits.
  • Target one certification in the next 12 months.

Before you leave, write down one action you'll take in the next 7 days. That's where the journey starts.

Key takeaways & questions

  • Red tests, Blue defends, Purple connects both — all are needed to secure real orgs.
  • Tools and commands are just force multipliers on top of strong fundamentals and thinking.
  • There are many paths in, and the industry demand is high if you put in consistent work.
